## The challenge of recognizing child speech

Most automatic speech recognition (ASR) systems are trained on adult speech. Children's voices are fundamentally different — higher pitch, more variable pronunciation, shorter utterances, and frequent disfluencies that are developmentally normal rather than errors by any means. Off-the-shelf ASR models struggle with pediatric speech, and they fail even harder when the child *has* a speech disorder — which is exactly the population we need to serve.

Building Wulo required us to rethink speech recognition from the ground up for this use case. We needed models that could:

- Accurately transcribe children's speech across ages 3–12
- Distinguish between developmentally appropriate variation and clinically significant errors
- Evaluate pronunciation at the **phoneme level**, not just word level
- Operate in real time with low enough latency to feel conversational

We achieved this through a combination of fine-tuned ASR models trained on pediatric speech corpora, custom phoneme alignment pipelines, and pronunciation scoring models calibrated against speech-language pathologist evaluations.

## Phoneme-level pronunciation evaluation

Standard ASR asks: "What word did the person say?" Wulo asks a different question: "How well did the child produce the target sound?"

This requires phoneme-level analysis. When a child attempts the word "rabbit," we don't just check whether the ASR transcribes "rabbit" — we evaluate whether the /r/ phoneme was produced correctly. This is a fundamentally harder problem, because we need to:

1. **Segment the audio** into phoneme-level windows
2. **Extract acoustic features** relevant to the target sound (formant frequencies, spectral characteristics, duration)
3. **Score the production** against a model of correct production, accounting for the child's age and developmental stage
4. **Classify the error type** when production is incorrect (substitution, omission, distortion, addition)

The error classification is what makes Wulo's feedback actionable. Knowing that a child substituted /w/ for /r/ ("wabbit") leads to very different therapeutic guidance than knowing they distorted the /r/ (a partially correct tongue position). Our models can make these distinctions in real time.

## Designing effective feedback for kids

Getting the speech recognition right is only half the challenge. The other half is delivering feedback that actually helps a child improve — without frustrating them or undermining their confidence.

We worked with licensed speech-language pathologists to design Wulo's feedback patterns:

- **Positive reinforcement first** — Every attempt gets acknowledged. "Great try!" or "I heard you working hard on that sound!" before any corrective feedback.
- **Specific, actionable cues** — Instead of "Try again," Wulo gives placement cues: "Try putting your tongue up behind your top teeth" or "Make the sound longer, like a snake: sssss."
- **Modeling** — The agent produces the target sound clearly so the child can hear the target before attempting again.
- **Graduated scaffolding** — If a child struggles, the exercise simplifies: word level → syllable level → isolation. If they succeed, it progresses: isolation → syllable → word → phrase → sentence.
- **Celebration of progress** — When a child masters a sound at a new level, the agent celebrates genuinely. Small wins matter.

The tone is always warm, patient, and encouraging. The agent never expresses frustration, never rushes, and never makes a child feel like they've failed.

## Adaptive difficulty and session flow

Every child's speech profile is different. Some struggle with /r/ but produce /s/ perfectly. Others have difficulty with entire sound classes (e.g., fricatives). Wulo's session engine adapts in real time:

- **Within a session**: If a child is consistently producing a sound correctly, the agent increases complexity (isolation → words → phrases). If they're struggling, it provides more scaffolding and reduces complexity.
- **Across sessions**: Wulo tracks which sounds a child has practiced, how they've performed, and what progression level they've reached. Each new session picks up where the last one left off.
- **Therapist-aligned goals**: When a therapist sets specific targets (e.g., "Focus on /r/ blends in initial position"), Wulo's session planner prioritizes those targets while maintaining engagement through variety.

Sessions are designed to be 10–15 minutes — long enough for meaningful practice, short enough to maintain a young child's attention. The agent uses varied exercise types (sound games, listening challenges, word repetition, sentence completion) to keep the experience engaging.

Building voice agents for pediatric articulation is one of the hardest applications of speech AI. The stakes are high — these are children's communication skills and confidence. But done well, it can provide the consistent, structured practice that research shows makes the biggest difference in therapy outcomes.
